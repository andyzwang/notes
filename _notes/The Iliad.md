***The Iliad*** is an epic poem from the 8th century BCE, attributed to [[Homer]]. As one of the oldest surviving pieces of literature, it is a foundational text for [[Ancient political thought|ancient political thought]].

*The Iliad* tells a story from the 10th year of the Trojan War: the conflict between [[Achilles]] and [[Agamemnon]], and the consequences of Achilles’ withdrawal from battle. While the poem is primarily set during war, it is also a deeply political work: how can [[Justice|justice]] exist in a world governed by personal relationships, honor, and force — and not stable norms?

*N.B. This note uses the translation by Lattimore, as found on the [Chicago Homer Project](https://homer.library.northwestern.edu).*

## Background

The Trojan War begins after Paris, prince of Troy, takes Helen from Menelaus, king of Sparta. In turn, the Achaean kings lead an expedition against Troy, commanded by [[Agamemnon]], king of Mycenae and brother of Menelaus.

By the time the poem begins, the war has already lasted nearly ten years. While the Achaeans have not yet captured Troy, they have raided surrounding towns and taken captives as prizes. Among these captives are Chryseis — awarded to Agamemnon — and Briseis — awarded to Achilles.

Book 1 begins when Chyseis' father, a priest of Apollo, comes to ransom his daughter. However, Agamemnon refuses: Apollo, defending the honor of his priest, sends a plague through the Greek armies.[^1] After nine days, Achilles calls for an assembly, where Agamemnon agrees to return Chyseis but takes Briseis from Achilles as compensation. Dishonored in front of the gathered forces, Achilles vows to no longer fight for Agamemnon.
