***Works and Days*** (Ἔργα καὶ Ἡμέραι) is a poem by [[Hesiod]], written around 700 BCE. In the poem — part farmer's almanac, part mythology, and part moral education — Hesiod instructs his brother Perses on how he should live his life.

In the poem, Hesiod states that he and his brother are the heirs of a farm together. However, Perses has bribed a judge in his favor to obtain Hesiod's portion as well. Instead of giving his brother money or property, which Hesiod sees as a futile effort, he decides to teach him the virtues of work and impact his brotherly wisdom.

## Synopsis

### Invocation

The poem begins with an invocation to the muses of Pieria, and to their father, Zeus, who controls the fates of mankind.

> Through him mortal men are famed or un-famed, sung or unsung alike, as great Zeus wills. For easily he makes strong, and easily he brings the strong man low; easily he humbles the proud and raises the obscure, and easily he straightens the crooked and blasts the proud.[^1]

After this appeal to Zeus — he who "make[s] judgments straight with righteousness" — Hesiod begins his undertaking to "tell of true things" to his brother, Perses.
### Two forms of strife

While *Works and Days* concerns itself with human affairs, Hesiod begins the poem by referencing the cosmic lineage in *[[Theogony]]*. There are two forms of Strife (Ἔρις) on earth: one "fosters evil war and battle," while the other "stirs up even the shiftless to toil."

> For a man grows eager to work when he considers his neighbor, a rich man who hastens to plough and plant and put his house in good order; and neighbor vies with his neighbor as he hurries after wealth. This Strife is wholesome for men. And potter is angry with potter, and craftsman with craftsman, and beggar is jealous of beggar, and minstrel of minstrel.[^2]

Hesiod encourages Perses to avoid taking delight in strife, telling him to not "peep and peer and listen to the wrangles of the court-house." Instead, Hesiod suggests, he should focus on working for his livelihood.
### Bribe-swallowing lords

> Nay, let us settle our dispute here with true judgement which is of Zeus and is perfect.[^3]

Hesiod gets to business by revealing that they had "already divided our inheritance," but Perses was able to claim more than his fair share through influencing "bribe-swallowing lords who love to judge such a cause as this."

These judges, Hesiod says, "know not how much more the half is than the whole, nor what great advantage there is in mallow and asphodel."[^4] To further convince Perses of the necessity of work — as well as the cosmic justice that underlies it — Hesiod begins his mythology.
### Prometheus and Pandora

Upset that [[Prometheus]] stole fire for humanity, [[Zeus]] decides that he will "give men as the price for fire an evil thing."[^5] He orders the gods to create Pandora, a beautiful woman who is endowed with gifts from all of the Olympians. Hermes brings Pandora to Epimetheus (Prometheus's brother) as a gift, and, despite Prometheus's warning to never accept a gift from Zeus, he accepts. Pandora opens a jar that releases sickness, toil, misery, and "countless plagues" into a world previously free of suffering. Only Hope remains within the lid — "by the will of Aegis-holding Zeus"[^6] — leaving humanity subject to hardship for all eternity.

Perhaps as a note to his brother, Hesiod concludes that "there is no way to escape the will of Zeus."

### Myth of the ages

Next, Hesiod tells the Myth of the Ages, a story of the progression of humans from near-divine existence to the current period, which is beset by trouble and toil.

#### Golden age

> And they lived like gods without sorrow of heart, remote and free from toil and grief: miserable age rested not on them; but with legs and arms never failing they made merry with feasting beyond the reach of all evils.[^7]

The golden age dates to the time of [[Cronos]]. Humans had a near-godly existence, and the plentiful resources of the earth meant peace among all men. When humans died, it was as though they went to sleep; after this age came to a close, the golden men became "pure spirits dwelling on the earth," protecting mankind from evils and granting them wealth.[^8]

#### Silver age

This generation was "less noble by far," as they were "like the golden race neither in body nor in spirit."[^8] As "utter simpletons," Men lived with their mothers for a hundred years; afterwards, they would live but short lives "because of their foolishness." Silver men could "not keep from sinning and from wronging one another," and they refused to honor and obey the gods. Thus, Zeus destroyed the race, and they became spirits of the underworld.

#### Bronze age

> They loved the lamentable works of Ares and deeds of violence; they ate no bread, but were hard of heart like adamant, fearful men. Great was their strength and unconquerable the arms which grew from their shoulders on their strong limbs.

The men of the Bronze Age were "terrible and strong," with bronze armor, houses, and weapons. Their warlike disposition meant that they destroyed each other, leaving no legacy.  

#### Heroic age

The race of heroes — the only without a metal in its name — was "nobler and more righteous." It is also the only age that improves on its preceding one. Although the men of this age were demi-gods, they fell in war, such as at [[Thebes]] and [[The Iliad|Troy]]. After their deaths, they live in the islands of the blessed, where "the grain-giving earth bears honey-sweet fruit flourishing thrice a year."[^9]

#### Iron age

> For now truly is a race of iron, and men never rest from labour and sorrow by day, and from perishing by night; and the gods shall lay sore trouble upon them.[^10]

Finally, Hesiod laments that he is alive during the so-called Iron Age (wishing that he "either had died before or been born afterwards"). Zeus will also destroy this race, he predicts. Society at that point will have fallen into total moral and religious decline. Children will dishonor their parents, oaths will be broken, and strength will overcome justice.

> There will be no favor for the man who keeps his oath or for the just or for the good; but rather men will praise the evil-doer and his violent dealing. Strength will be right and reverence will cease to be.
